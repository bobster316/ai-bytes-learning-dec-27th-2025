Lesson core

The Anatomy of a Perfect Prompt

Every effective prompt is built from exactly four parts. Omit any one and the AI has to guess — and it will guess wrong.

Role — Part 1 of 4

Give it a character to play

An AI without a role is like an actor without a script — capable of anything, committed to nothing. When you assign a role, you're activating a specific slice of the model's training data. "You are a senior UX researcher" immediately shifts tone, vocabulary, and depth.

Think of roles as unlocking modes. A "cynical editor" cuts ruthlessly. A "patient teacher for beginners" avoids jargon entirely. The role shapes every sentence that follows.

"You are a senior UX researcher with 10 years of experience in fintech. You speak plainly, question assumptions, and always tie recommendations to user evidence."
Context — Part 2 of 4

Set the scene completely

AI cannot read your mind. Without context, it assumes the most average possible scenario — and produces the most average possible answer. Context tells it: who the audience is, what they know, what the output is for, and what constraints apply.

A useful test: imagine handing your prompt to a brilliant new contractor on their first day. What would they need to know to produce exactly what you want? That's your context.

"My audience is non-technical stakeholders at a Series B startup. They have 5 minutes and no AI background. The output will be shown on slides, not printed."
Task — Part 3 of 4

State exactly what you want

Vague tasks produce vague results — every time, without exception. "Help me with my website" could mean write copy, fix code, or redesign the layout. The AI picks one interpretation and runs. You spend three follow-ups correcting a direction you never intended.

A strong task has three components: a clear action verb (write, summarise, critique), a specific scope (this paragraph, these three points), and a success criterion.

"Write a 3-paragraph executive summary: (1) problem, (2) solution, (3) why now. Each paragraph max 4 sentences."
Format — Part 4 of 4

Tell it how to package the answer

Without format instructions, AI defaults to multi-section essays with headers or endless bullet lists. Neither is what most people actually need. Format instructions tell the model how to present its thinking — not what to think.

This is the lowest-effort, highest-impact part of your prompt. "Reply in plain English, no bullet points, under 150 words" transforms a sprawling response into something immediately usable — in one sentence.

"Format: 3 short paragraphs. Plain English. No jargon, no bullet points. End with one specific action the reader should take today."

Real-world impact

This isn't theory. It changes what you get — every single time.

In a study by researchers at the University of Chicago, the quality difference between poorly-written and well-structured prompts was indistinguishable from the difference between entry-level and expert-level human writing. Same model. Different input. Completely different output.

Professionals who learn to prompt well describe it as gaining a new hire who's available instantly, never moody, never too tired, and gets sharper every time you give clearer instructions. The bottleneck is never the AI — it's always the quality of the brief.

Marketers use structured prompts to cut content creation time by 60%. Developers use them to generate code that actually matches their architecture. Managers use them to turn rough notes into polished memos in seconds. The skill is the same in every case: describe what you need precisely enough that there's only one way to interpret it.

The rule of thumb: if a human assistant would need to ask you a clarifying question before starting, your prompt is missing something.

Advanced techniques

Chain of Thought

Ask the AI to "think step by step" before answering. This forces it to reason through the problem visibly — and dramatically improves accuracy on anything complex. It was one of the most significant prompt engineering discoveries of 2022, and it still works reliably today across every major model.

The reason it works: by externalising its reasoning, the model catches its own errors before committing to an answer. Instead of jumping to a conclusion, it builds one. You get transparency and accuracy in one simple instruction.

"Think through this step by step before giving your final answer. First, identify the core problem. Then list 3 possible approaches. Then recommend the best one with your reasoning."
Few-Shot Prompting

Show the AI 2–3 examples of what you want before asking it to produce the real thing. It learns your style, tone, and format from the examples — no lengthy instructions needed.

This is especially powerful when you have a distinctive voice or format that's hard to describe in words. Instead of explaining the pattern, you demonstrate it. The model reverse-engineers it and applies it consistently to your real input.

Input:  "quarterly results up 12%"
Output: "Q3 results beat expectations with 12% growth."

Input:  "new feature launched in beta"
Output: "Beta launch signals our fastest product cycle yet."

Persona Stacking

Combine multiple expert lenses into one role. Instead of "be an expert", give the AI a compound identity — and watch the quality of analysis multiply. Each persona brings different priorities, blind spots, and insights.

A VC asks "will this scale?" A PM asks "will users actually use this?" A growth hacker asks "where's the fastest traction?" Getting all three perspectives at once is more valuable than any single expert opinion.

"You are simultaneously:
(1) a VC who has backed 50 SaaS companies
(2) a PM who shipped 3 failed products
(3) a growth hacker who reached $10M ARR

Evaluate this idea from all three perspectives."
Negative Constraints

Tell the AI what NOT to do. This is often more powerful than positive instructions — it eliminates the default behaviours that dilute output quality. AI models have deeply ingrained habits: starting sentences with "I", adding unnecessary bullet points, ending with a summary paragraph nobody asked for.

Negative constraints cut through these defaults directly. One well-placed "do not" instruction can transform a mediocre, formulaic response into something clean, direct, and genuinely useful.

"CONSTRAINTS — do not:
• Use bullet points
• Start sentences with 'I'
• Use the word 'leverage'
• Summarise before answering
• Add a conclusion paragraph
Just answer directly."
