Prompt Engineering · Lesson 01

The difference between a mediocre answer and an expert one is your question.

12 min  ·  3 concepts  ·  No prior experience needed

Concept 01

Four parts. Every great prompt has them.

You are a senior UX researcher with 10 years of B2B SaaS experience.ROLE — who the AI should be

We are redesigning our onboarding flow for enterprise clients.CONTEXT — the situation

Identify the three most common failure points and suggest one specific fix for each.TASK — what you want done

Format as a numbered list. Under 60 words each. No buzzwords.FORMAT — how to respond

Without role

The model responds as a generic assistant — broad advice, vague suggestions, no domain depth. Useful to nobody in particular.

With role

The model draws on specific UX frameworks, enterprise SaaS patterns, and a decade of implied experience. Advice is immediately actionable.

Concept 02

The exact same question. Two completely different results.

Before
Help me write an email to my team about the project delay.

Sure! Here's an email you could send:

"Hi team, I wanted to let you know that the project is running a bit behind schedule. We'll keep you updated. Thanks for your understanding."

Response quality 18%
After
You are an experienced engineering manager. Write a 3-paragraph email to a 12-person product team explaining a 2-week delay on the mobile checkout feature. Tone: honest, calm, solution-focused. Include: the cause (third-party API integration), what's changed in the plan, and next check-in date.

Subject: Mobile Checkout — Updated Timeline + Plan

"Team — I want to be straight with you about where we stand…" [precise, structured, credible — 3 paragraphs as requested, with specific next steps and a calendar invite placeholder]

Response quality 94%

Quick check

You send "Help me write an email." What's the most likely problem?

Concept 03

Three techniques that separate experts from everyone else.

01
Chain of Thought Reasoning

Ask the model to think step by step before giving a final answer. This dramatically reduces errors on anything involving logic, maths, or multi-step decisions — because the model externalises its reasoning rather than jumping straight to a conclusion.

Example "Before answering, work through this step by step. First identify the key variables, then reason through each one, then give your final recommendation."
02
Persona Anchoring Voice

Assign a specific persona with real stakes — not just a job title, but a point of view, constraints, and expertise level. "A CFO who is sceptical of AI" produces very different analysis than "an AI enthusiast." The more specific the persona, the more useful the lens.

Example "You are a Series B CFO who has seen three failed digital transformation projects. Review this proposal with healthy scepticism."
03
Constraint Stacking Precision

Add explicit constraints to prevent the vague, padded output that plagues unstructured prompts. Word limits, banned phrases, required structure, and tone boundaries all reduce the model's degrees of freedom — forcing it to be precise rather than comprehensive.

Example "Under 80 words. No passive voice. No phrases like 'it's important to note'. Three bullet points only. Start with the action, not the context."
