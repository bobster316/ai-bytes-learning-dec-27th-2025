FOUNDATION

The shift from retrieval to creation

Traditional software follows instructions written by humans. If you ask a calculator to add two numbers, it applies a formula. If you ask a search engine for information, it retrieves documents from an index. Neither system creates — they retrieve or compute.

Generative AI breaks that pattern entirely. These models are trained on enormous quantities of human-produced content — text, images, code — and learn the statistical patterns that underlie all of it. When prompted, they do not look up an answer. They generate one, token by token, based on what their training suggests should come next.

KEY INSIGHT

A generative model is not reasoning from first principles — it is extrapolating from patterns. Understanding this distinction is foundational to using these tools effectively and critically.

This probabilistic nature means outputs vary. Run the same prompt twice and you get different results. This is shaped by a parameter called temperature — higher values produce more creative outputs, lower values produce more conservative ones.

The implications are profound. A generative model can write code it has never seen before, compose music in the style of a composer it studied, or generate images of places that do not exist. The constraint is not imagination — it is the boundaries of its training distribution.

MODEL FAMILIES

Three Families of Generative AI

Each architecture approaches creation differently — and excels in different domains.

LANGUAGE

Large Language Models

Transformer-based models trained to predict the next token in a sequence. They power chatbots, code generators, and writing assistants. GPT-4, Gemini, and Claude are all LLMs.

IMAGES

Diffusion Models

These models learn to reverse a noise-adding process. Starting from pure noise, they iteratively denoise until a coherent image emerges. Stable Diffusion and DALL-E 3 use this approach.

→ Best for: image synthesis, art, design generation

ADVERSARIAL

Generative Adversarial Networks

Two neural networks compete: a generator creates samples, a discriminator judges them. Over time, the generator improves to fool the discriminator. GANs excel at hyper-realistic image generation.

→ Best for: deepfakes, synthetic data, style transfer

DEEP STRUCTURE

How a Transformer Works

SCROLL TO STEP THROUGH THE ARCHITECTURE

STEP 01

Tokenisation

Before a model can process your prompt, it splits text into tokens — small units that might be a word, part of a word, or a single character. “Unbelievable” might become [“Un”, “believ”, “able”]. This is why models have a “context window” measured in tokens, not words.

→ “Hello world” ≈ [“Hello”, “ world”] = 2 tokens

STEP 02

Embedding

Each token is converted into a high-dimensional vector — a list of numbers that encodes its meaning. Words with similar meanings cluster together in this vector space. “King” and “Queen” are near each other; “King” and “Carrot” are far apart. This representation preserves semantic relationships across the vocabulary.

→ “cat” → [0.23, −0.41, 0.88, …] (768 dimensions)

STEP 03

Attention Mechanism

The attention mechanism lets each token “look at” every other token in the sequence and weigh how relevant they are. This is the breakthrough that makes transformers powerful — it captures long-range dependencies that earlier architectures missed. Multi-head attention runs this process across many parallel views simultaneously.

→ “The bank by the river was steep” — “bank” attends to “river” to resolve ambiguity

STEP 04

Output Generation

The final layer produces a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary — for each possible next token, what is the likelihood it should come next? The model samples from this distribution. Temperature controls how peaked or spread out this distribution is before sampling occurs.

→ temp=0.1: picks the top token almost always  |  temp=1.0: samples more freely

BEFORE YOU CONTINUE

What does a language model actually do when generating text?

Correct. Language models are autoregressive — they predict one token at a time, conditioning each prediction on everything that came before it. This is why they produce long, coherent text: each step informs the next. They are not searching, retrieving, or selecting — they are generating.

REAL WORLD

Generative AI Across Industries

The same underlying technology reshapes very different workflows.

Clinical Documentation at Scale

Generative AI is reshaping clinical documentation by automatically summarising patient records, drafting discharge notes, and generating referral letters from consultation transcripts. Physicians dictate naturally; the model produces structured documentation. This reduces administrative burden by up to 40%, giving clinicians more time with patients rather than paperwork.

Automated Analyst Intelligence

Investment banks use generative models to draft equity research summaries, synthesise earnings call transcripts, and generate risk commentary for portfolios. Tasks that once required junior analysts six hours now complete in minutes. The model surfaces relevant signals; the analyst adds judgement and oversight.

Personalised Learning at Every Level

Educational platforms use generative AI to adapt content to individual learners — explaining the same concept multiple ways, generating practice problems at the right difficulty, and providing instant feedback on written responses. Adaptive quizzes update in real time based on which concepts a student has mastered.

Content at the Speed of Thought

Media organisations use generative AI to draft scripts, generate ad copy variants, create image assets, and repurpose long-form content into social snippets. A single journalist’s article can become ten tweet threads, a LinkedIn post, a newsletter summary, and a podcast script — all in minutes, with human review.

Contract Intelligence

Law firms use generative AI to draft standard contract clauses, extract key obligations from lengthy agreements, and flag unusual provisions in due diligence reviews. What previously required paralegal hours of document review now completes in a fraction of the time. Lawyers focus on strategy, negotiation, and judgement.

model
Which LLM to use — different models have different capabilities, speeds, and costs.
messages
Conversation history as an array. “system” sets behaviour; “user” is your prompt. Order matters.
temperature
0 = deterministic (always picks the top token). 1 = creative (samples more freely). 0.7 is the typical balanced setting.
max_tokens
Output length cap. 200 tokens ≈ 150 words. Higher values cost more; lower values truncate long responses.

⚠ COMMON MISTAKE

Treating AI output as fact. Generative models hallucinate — they produce confident-sounding text that is factually wrong. Always verify claims against authoritative sources, especially in high-stakes domains such as medicine, law, or finance.

IF YOU REMEMBER ONLY THREE THINGS

1

Generative AI creates — it doesn’t retrieve

Unlike search engines or databases, generative models produce new content based on learned patterns. Every output is synthesised, not fetched from a stored answer.

2

Probability, not understanding, drives output

These models do not comprehend meaning as humans do. They operate on statistical patterns over tokens. Impressive outputs can coexist with fundamental factual errors.

3

Prompt quality determines output quality

Vague prompts produce vague results. Specificity about audience, format, tone, and scope consistently produces better outputs — a skill called prompt engineering.

GLOSSARY

Key Terms

Plain-English definitions for the concepts in this lesson.

A neural network trained on vast text corpora to predict the next token in a sequence. LLMs like GPT-4, Gemini, and Claude can generate coherent, contextually appropriate text across an enormous range of tasks — from coding to creative writing — because they have learned the statistical structure of human language at scale.
The process of splitting text into smaller units (tokens) before it can be processed by a language model. Tokens are typically sub-word units — common words become single tokens while rare words are split into multiple parts. A model’s “context window” is measured in tokens, not words.
The core innovation in transformer architectures. Attention allows every token to “attend to” every other token in the sequence, computing relevance weights. This enables models to capture long-range dependencies in text — understanding that “it” in a long sentence refers to a noun mentioned many words earlier.
A parameter that controls the randomness of a generative model’s output by scaling the probability distribution before sampling. Temperature 0 always picks the most probable next token (deterministic, repetitive). Temperature 1 samples from the full distribution (more creative, occasionally surprising or incoherent).
When a generative model produces factually incorrect information stated with apparent confidence. Hallucinations occur because models generate based on statistical patterns rather than verified knowledge — they optimise for plausibility, not truth. More common with obscure facts, recent events, and numerical claims.
